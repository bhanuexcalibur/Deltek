Part 01 The brief
Costpoint has shipped more product in 18 months than it probably did in the previous five years. The market hasn't caught up yet. That's the job.

Deltek is in a genuinely unusual position. Costpoint is the default ERP for US government contractors, full stop. There's no meaningful ERP competitor who knows the FAR, DCAA, CMMC stack the way Costpoint does. That kind of market position is rare, and it gives the marketing team permission to move on its own terms.

But that same dominance creates a quieter problem: when customers feel locked in, they stop paying close attention to new capabilities. Costpoint users trust the product for compliance. They don't necessarily trust it for AI. Those are different buying decisions, and they require different stories.

In 2025, Deltek's own Clarity study found that 45% of GovCon firms are now using AI in their operations, up 10 points in a year. That's not a laggard audience anymore. These firms are evaluating AI tools actively. The PMM's job is to make sure that when a Costpoint user asks "can our ERP do this?", the answer is already obvious to them.

That means three things: positioning Dela as a concrete operational tool rather than a platform-level promise, connecting each AI feature to the compliance and financial outcomes GovCon leadership actually cares about, and building enablement that helps Sales explain this to a DCAA-first CFO without making it sound like a pitch from a software company.

Part 02 Messaging audit: Costpoint AI

How Dela is currently positioned vs. how it should be

Based on current Deltek.com copy, product release notes, and ProjectCon 2025 materials

Current

"A smarter, sleeker experience that completely reimagines how government contractors interact with Costpoint."

Reframe

"The same Costpoint your team relies on, now built around how you actually work. Find anything faster, train new hires quicker, and stop losing time to navigation."

The issue with "completely reimagines": For a GovCon finance team that has run Costpoint for a decade, "completely reimagined" sounds like retraining risk, not a benefit. The reframe keeps the familiarity (same Costpoint) while making the value concrete: speed, onboarding, less friction. Same product, different emotional register.
Current

"Dela Agents are task-specific, intelligent automation components that take action on behalf of the user."

Reframe

"Expense reports, timesheet approvals, contract records from documents. The things that clog up your accounting team's Friday afternoon. Dela handles them. Your team doesn't have to."

The issue with "take action on behalf of the user": This is product documentation language, not buyer language. GovCon buyers don't think in terms of "automation components." They think in terms of specific tasks that eat hours. The reframe names those tasks directly: expense reports, timesheet approvals, contract records. Concrete beats architectural every time with this audience.
Current

"Costpoint GCCM has achieved FedRAMP Moderate Equivalency and supports DFARS 252.204-7012 and CMMC compliance."

Reframe

"If your DoD contract requires CMMC, Costpoint GCCM is already assessed. That's one less thing your team has to prove. The assessment is done. The documentation is yours."

Why this matters: GovCon compliance officers aren't asking "does this meet DFARS?" They're asking "what do I have to do to demonstrate compliance before the audit?" The reframe answers the operational question, not the technical one. "The assessment is done" is the line that ends a procurement conversation, not the regulation number.
Part 03 GTM plan: Dela Agents adoption

Driving adoption of Dela Agents among existing Costpoint customers

Launch scenario  /  Q3 2025 feature release  /  Existing customer base

Customer adoption play

The problem we're solving

Dela Agents shipped. The feature can automate timesheet approvals, expense reports, and contract record creation. Most existing Costpoint customers don't know it exists, and the ones who've seen the release notes don't know what it means for their team's day-to-day work.

The gap isn't awareness at the product level. It's awareness at the outcomes level. "Dela Agents are available" is not the same as "your accounting team gets two hours back every Friday."

Target segment

Mid-size GovCon firms (200-1,000 employees) currently on Costpoint cloud. Finance and operations leads who own timekeeping and expense workflows. Not the IT buyer. Not the C-suite. The person who is personally annoyed by the Friday afternoon approval pile-up.

Secondary: Customer Success teams who can surface adoption data and flag accounts where feature usage is near zero.

Positioning angle

Don't lead with "AI." Lead with the task. GovCon buyers have compliance anxiety around AI that's real and valid. Leading with "Dela Agents are AI-powered" triggers that anxiety before you've established trust.

Instead: "Your timesheet approvals are already in Costpoint. Dela can run them for you. Same process, same compliance controls, no manual steps." AI is in the fine print, not the headline. Trust first, capability second.

Channel and tactics

1. In-product prompts at the specific moment a user is about to do the manual version of the task Dela can automate. "Dela can do this for you" at the expense report screen beats any email campaign.

2. Customer Success-led activation for the top 100 accounts by usage gap. CS reaches out with a specific number: "Based on your account data, your team spends roughly X hours per month on manual approvals that Dela can handle."

3. Short-form video for user conferences and email showing a real workflow: expense report from upload to approval in under a minute.

30-60-90 day execution timeline

Days 1-30
Brief and align. Work with CS to pull activation data: which account segments have zero Dela usage? Build the ICP list for the activation outreach. Write the in-product prompt copy with PM. Get the 60-second workflow video scripted and into production. Land the positioning language across Sales, CS, and the web page.
Days 31-60
Run the activation campaign. In-product prompts live. CS sends activation emails to the targeted segment using the data-backed framing. Host one 30-minute customer webinar showing the real workflow, not slides. Collect objections from CS and Sales calls and build a live objection-handling card for the field.
Days 61-90
Measure and double down. Pull Dela Agents feature activation rate vs baseline. Talk to 5 customers who adopted and 5 who didn't. Find out what the actual hesitation was. Write a short case study from the fastest adoption story and use it in the next round of CS outreach. Feed insights back to PM on what "safe AI" means to this audience.

How I'd measure success

Primary
30%
Dela Agents activation rate in target segment within 90 days (from near-zero baseline)
Secondary
5+
Customer interviews completed. Qualitative data matters as much as activation numbers for this feature.
Enablement
100%
Sales and CS reps able to demo Dela Agents in a discovery call without a PMM in the room
Part 04 Buyer map: who's actually in the room

Costpoint has three distinct buyer types who all have to say yes in the same deal. The problem is that Deltek's current messaging largely speaks to one of them at a time. A CFO and a DCAA auditor and a Project Manager are all using Costpoint for different things, under completely different pressures. The copy needs to work for all three.

Part 05 Copy and enablement samples
Landing page hero  /  Dela Agents feature page Landing page
Your team doesn't have to touch every expense report anymore.
The approvals. The timesheets. The contract records that take an afternoon to create from a single document. Dela does these now, inside the same Costpoint workflows your team already knows, with the same compliance controls already in place. Nothing new to audit. Nothing new to approve. Just fewer things on the list.
Why this works: GovCon buyers have a strong prior that "new AI features" mean new workflows to manage and new things to explain to auditors. This copy eliminates that concern in the first sentence ("same Costpoint workflows," "same compliance controls"). The benefit is scoped to concrete tasks they recognize. The word "AI" doesn't appear once, because it doesn't need to.
Customer email  /  Dela Agents activation outreach for existing accounts Email
There's a feature in your Costpoint account your team probably hasn't turned on yet.
Based on your account data, your team is still manually processing expense reports and timesheet approvals the same way you were 18 months ago. That's not a problem exactly, it's just time that could be somewhere else.

Costpoint added the ability to automate both of those workflows last quarter. It takes about 20 minutes to configure. Once it's on, your accounting team doesn't touch routine approvals anymore unless something is flagged. It works within the compliance controls you already have in place, so there's nothing new to add to your audit package.

If you want a 15-minute walkthrough to see if it fits how your team works, your CSM can show you.
Why this works: This email leads with what the customer already has ("in your Costpoint account"), not what Deltek built. It uses account data to make the outreach feel specific, not broadcast. "20 minutes to configure" addresses the adoption anxiety. And "nothing new to add to your audit package" is the single most important thing you can say to a GovCon finance team before they agree to enable a new feature.
Internal sales card  /  Competitive objection: "We don't need AI in our ERP" Sales enablement
When they say "we're not really looking at AI tools right now"
Don't push on AI. Ask one question: "When you close out a contract or process a batch of expense reports, what does that look like for your accounting team on a Friday afternoon?" Then let them answer. If the answer is "it's a pain" or "we have a person who handles that," the follow-up writes itself: "What we're talking about isn't really an AI project. It's just automating the specific steps in Costpoint your team is already doing manually. The controls don't change. The audit trail doesn't change. The only thing that changes is your team doesn't have to do it themselves."
Why this works: GovCon buyers who push back on AI almost always have a compliance concern underneath, not a capability concern. This card redirects the conversation away from "AI" entirely and anchors it on a workflow problem the prospect has already admitted to. The rep is not arguing for AI. They're describing an automation that the prospect just described needing. The compliance language at the end ("controls don't change, audit trail doesn't change") removes the residual objection without raising it as an issue.
Internal brief  /  AI-assisted GTM: how I'd actually use AI in this role Process note
How I use AI to work faster without the output showing it
The JD specifically asks for a candidate who can demonstrate AI use in GTM execution. Here's what that looks like in practice for me.

Research compression: Before any messaging project, I use Claude to pull and synthesize public customer feedback from G2, Capterra, and Reddit threads. What used to take two days of reading now takes 90 minutes of synthesis and pattern-spotting. The insight quality is higher because I'm reading more signal.

Copy iteration: I draft in my own voice first, then use AI to stress-test the draft: "read this as a DCAA auditor" or "what's the objection a skeptical CFO has to this claim?" The AI doesn't write the copy. It plays the skeptic so I don't have to wait for Sales feedback to find the holes.

Enablement scaling: One strong interview becomes a battle card, a FAQ, a short talk track, and a social proof snippet, all in a single session. The PMM writes the original. AI handles the format variation.
Why I'm including this: The role asks specifically for a candidate who can walk through a GTM example that includes AI use. This is that example: real workflow changes, not theoretical ones. The output doesn't sound AI-generated because the thinking, structure, and positioning judgment are mine. AI handles the parts that don't require judgment so that my time goes to the parts that do.
